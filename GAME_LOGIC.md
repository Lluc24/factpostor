# 🕵️ FACTPOSTOR - Game Rules & Design

> *"A lie is just a fact that hasn't been believed yet."*

A social deduction party game for **3–10 players** where truth is weaponized, lies are dressed up in science clothing, and everyone ends up learning something — except possibly the wrong thing.

---

## What Is This Game?

**Factpostor** is a variant of the classic Impostor/Undercover word game. Instead of sharing a secret word, players are given **a random true fact** — except for the Impostors, who receive nothing but the knowledge that they are lying scoundrels.

The Impostors must invent a **plausible-sounding fake fact** on the spot and blend in with the honest players. Everyone then discusses and votes to expose them.

It's one part trivia night, one part improv comedy, and one part courtroom drama where the crime is *making something up about penguins*.

---

## Players & Roles

| Role | What They Know | What They Must Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Citizen** | A specific true fact | Talk about it convincingly and sniff out the fakes |
| **Factpostor** | Only that they are the Factpostor | Invent a believable fake fact and survive the vote |

### How many Impostors?

| Total Players | Recommended Impostors |
|---|---|
| 3–4 | 1 |
| 5–7 | 1–2 |
| 8–10 | 2–3 |

Having more than ⌊n/3⌋ Impostors makes the game too chaotic (or too easy for them). Keep it minority rule.

## What Is a "Fact"?

A fact is **a single, self-contained true statement** from any domain of knowledge. It should be:

- **Verifiable** — something that is genuinely, checkably true
- **Specific enough** to sound like it came from somewhere
- **Surprising or niche** — boring facts are bad camouflage *and* bad fun
- **Standalone** — it shouldn't require a second fact to make sense

### ✅ Good fact examples
- *"Cleopatra lived closer in time to the Moon landing than to the construction of the Great Pyramid."*
- *"Honey never spoils. Edible honey has been found in 3,000-year-old Egyptian tombs."*
- *"A group of flamingos is called a flamboyance."*
- *"Nintendo was founded in 1889 as a playing card company."*
- *"Sharks are older than trees. Sharks appeared ~450M years ago; trees ~350M years ago."*

### ❌ Bad fact examples
- *"Water is wet."* — Too well-known, no discussion value.
- *"Some scientists believe dark matter exists."* — Too vague.
- *"The economy of Brazil is complex."* — Not a fact, just vibes.

## Game Setup

There is no Fact Master. **The app is the Fact Master.** Every player is a participant.

### 1. Start a Session
One player opens the app and creates a new game session. They enter the names (or nicknames) of all participants. The app:

- Picks a random true fact for the round
- Randomly assigns roles (Citizen or Factpostor) to each player
- Locks everything until the reveal phase begins

No player sees any other player's assignment. The app knows everything and says nothing until asked.

### 2. The Reveal — Passing the Phone
The phone is passed around the table, one player at a time. Each player:

1. Takes the phone
2. Taps **"Show my role"**
3. Reads their screen privately (no peeking!)
4. Taps **"Done — I've seen it"** to confirm and hide the content
5. Passes the phone to the next player

- **Citizens see:** their role and the fact.
- **Factpostors see:** their role and nothing else.

> ⚠️ **Critical UX requirement:** After a player taps "Done", the screen must immediately return to a neutral state (e.g., just the game logo or a "Pass to the next player" prompt) before the phone changes hands. The fact must never be visible while the phone is in transit.

### 3. Optional: The Warm-Up Moment
Once everyone has seen their role, give players **60 seconds of silence** to think. Citizens may want to riff on their fact. Factpostors need to construct their lie. Do not skip this step — it makes the performances much better.

## Gameplay: A Round

### Phase 1 — The Declarations (2–4 minutes)

Going around the table (random order recommended), each player states **one thing** related to their fact, without revealing the fact directly. Think of it as a hint, an anecdote, a tangent, or an elaboration.

**Citizens** should speak naturally about their true fact — adding context, expressing surprise, connecting it to something else.

**Factpostors** should do the same, but for their invented fact. They may:
- Reference a plausible-sounding source ("I read somewhere that...")
- Add fake specific detail ("I think it was discovered in the 1970s...")
- Piggyback on a Citizen's statement ("Right, which relates to what I know about...")

> ⚠️ **Important rule:** Players may **not** directly state their full fact during Phase 1. They hint and discuss. The full fact reveal happens only during Phase 3.

### Phase 2 — The Discussion (3–5 minutes)

Open discussion. Players may:
- Ask each other follow-up questions
- Challenge suspicious statements
- Form alliances and accusations
- Deliberately mislead others (Citizens may lie *about their behavior*, never about their fact)

A good discussion question: *"Can you tell us more specifically where that comes from?"*

Factpostors beware: Citizens are allowed to ask you to elaborate. You must keep elaborating.

### Phase 3 — The Revelation Circle

Each player, one by one, **states their full fact** (or full invented fact, for Factpostors). Citizens state theirs truthfully. Factpostors commit to their lie.

After all facts are stated, players have **one final silent minute** to update their suspicions.

### Phase 4 — The Vote

Every player (including Factpostors!) simultaneously points at who they think is a Factpostor — or raises their hand if voting for themselves as the MVP bluffer.

The player with the **most votes** is eliminated and their role is revealed.

- If a Factpostor is eliminated: Citizens score a point each, and the Factpostor scores 0.
- If a Citizen is eliminated: Each surviving Factpostor scores 2 points. The eliminated Citizen scores 0 and cries.
- **Ties:** Re-vote among tied players only. If still tied, no elimination this round (the chaos option) or flip a coin (the coward option).

## Winning & Scoring

The game is played over multiple rounds (recommended: **3–6 rounds per session**, agreed upon before starting).

### Points

| Event | Who Scores | Points |
|---|---|---|
| A Factpostor is correctly voted out | All Citizens | +1 each |
| A Citizen is incorrectly voted out | Each surviving Factpostor | +2 each |
| A Factpostor survives the full game | That Factpostor | +3 bonus |
| A Factpostor's invented fact is voted "most believable" by the group | That Factpostor | +1 bonus |
| A Citizen correctly names ALL Factpostors during Discussion | That Citizen | +2 bonus |

> 💡 The "most believable fake fact" vote is unofficial but **highly recommended** — it turns the post-round debrief into a comedy awards ceremony.

### End of Game

The player with the most cumulative points wins. In case of a tie, the winner is determined by:
1. Most successful Factpostor rounds
2. If still tied: dramatic staredown

## The Art of the Fake Fact (Factpostor Guide)

Being a good Factpostor is a skill. Here is the collected wisdom:

### Structural formula for a convincing fake fact
> `[Specific animal / country / historical figure / everyday object]` + `[surprising but plausible numerical claim or behavior]` + `[optional: vague scientific-sounding reason]`

**Example:** *"Octopuses have a dominant arm, typically the third one from the left, which they use for most complex tasks."*
(Plausible-sounding. Specific. Has that "I read it in New Scientist" energy.)

### The Golden Rules of Lying Here
1. **Be specific.** Vague claims are immediately suspicious. Real facts have weird numbers and names.
2. **Stay calm.** Nervous energy is a tell. You believe this fact. It's yours.
3. **Don't over-elaborate unless asked.** Unprompted over-explanation screams guilt.
4. **Piggyback on Citizens.** If a Citizen says something true, build your fake fact in the same general domain — it creates false coherence.
5. **React to others' facts.** Nod. Say "oh interesting, that connects to what I know." Perform curiosity.
6. **Never contradict a Citizen's true fact directly.** You don't know what their fact is. If you contradict it, they know you're lying.

### What NOT to do
- ❌ Say "I think..." or "I'm pretty sure..." — Citizens are *certain* of their fact.
- ❌ Pick something too fantastical — "Dolphins can see ultraviolet light and use it to find drugs" is memorable in the wrong way.
- ❌ Panic-pick a fact you've actually heard before — real obscure facts you half-remember will be said by a Citizen and expose you immediately.

## Variants & House Rules

### 🔥 Hard Mode — No Category
In standard play, the topic of the fact is usually inferable from discussion. In Hard Mode, the Fact Master deliberately picks facts from wildly different domains each round, maximizing the Factpostor's disorientation.

### 🧊 Cold Read Mode
Factpostors must state their fake fact **first**, before hearing any other player's statements. No piggybacking. Maximum terror.

### 🎭 Theme Rounds
All facts in a round are from the same category (e.g., "only biology facts," "only facts about the Roman Empire," "only facts about food"). Citizens know the theme. Factpostors are told the theme as well — leveling the playing field slightly but adding topical depth.

### 🤝 Team Mode
Players split into teams. Each team has at least one Factpostor. Teams score collectively. Useful for larger groups (8+).

### 📱 AI Player Mode
An AI (like Claude) can play as a Citizen or Factpostor. If playing as a Factpostor, instruct it:

```
You are playing Factpostor. You have not been given a fact. 
Invent one plausible-sounding fake fact and defend it throughout the game.
When asked to elaborate, elaborate. Stay in character. Never admit you are lying.
```

If playing as a Citizen, give it the true fact and tell it to discuss and defend it naturally.

## Web App Implementation Notes

This web application implements FACTPOSTOR as a **single-device, in-person game**. There is no multiplayer server, no accounts, no push notifications. One phone. One table. Everyone present.

### App Flow

```
[Host enters player names]
        ↓
[App picks facts + assigns roles silently]
        ↓
[Phone passed around: each player taps "Show my role", reads, taps "Done"]
        ↓
[Game plays out in real life: Declarations → Discussion → Reveal → Vote]
        ↓
[App reveals all roles and facts]
        ↓
[Option to start new round or new game]
```

### Key Features
- **Neutral Pass Screen**: Shows only the next player's name between role reveals
- **Private Role Reveal**: Tap to reveal, tap to hide mechanics ensure privacy
- **Random Assignment**: Roles and facts are randomly assigned each round
- **Curated Fact Database**: 160+ hand-vetted interesting facts
- **No Backend Required**: All state is local to the device
- **Mobile-First Design**: Optimized for passing a single phone around a table

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What if a Citizen's fact sounds fake?**
A: This is the best possible outcome. Treasure it. The Citizen must defend truth against the court of vibes.

**Q: Can Factpostors collaborate?**
A: Not officially — they don't know who the other Factpostors are. But if they find each other during Discussion, an unofficial non-verbal alliance is part of the chaos.

**Q: What if the Factpostor accidentally invents something that's true?**
A: This is called a **Ghost Fact** and is worth an honorary bonus point decided by group applause.

**Q: Can I reuse facts across sessions?**
A: Yes, but players who've heard the fact before must sit out or play as Factpostors that round. The game is ruined if someone already knows the truth.

**Q: What if everyone votes for the same person every round because they're bad at lying?**
A: Consider enabling Theme Rounds — a shared topic gives Factpostors more material to work with and levels the playing field for creative liars.
